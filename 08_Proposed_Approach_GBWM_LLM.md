# Proposed Approach: Goals-Based Wealth Management Integrated with Large Language Models

After reviewing the reference literature you provided, with particular attention to the sections on Goal-based Investing and Personalized Investing, I have chosen Goals-Based Wealth Management (GBWM) as my core quantitative framework, integrated with Large Language Models (LLMs) for enhanced personalization and interpretability.  I will shoe my rationale, design, and implementation plan in detail.

The central motivation for selecting GBWM is its direct alignment with the project's theme of personalized investing. Unlike traditional mean-variance optimization, which seeks a single optimal portfolio for all investors, GBWM starts from the premise that each investor holds multiple financial goals simultaneously, such as retirement savings, children's education funding, and home purchase, each with its own time horizon, target amount, and acceptable probability of achievement. The framework, as formalized by Das, Evans, and Ashvin (2020, 2022), decomposes the overall portfolio into independent "mental accounts," one for each goal, and optimizes each sub-portfolio to maximize the probability of achieving its specific target. This decomposition property is both theoretically elegant and computationally practical, as it transforms a high-dimensional multi-objective problem into a collection of lower-dimensional single-objective sub-problems. Each sub-problem employs an S-shaped utility function inspired by Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory, where investors exhibit risk-seeking behavior below the target amount (willing to take more risk to reach the goal) and risk-averse behavior above it (protecting accumulated gains). This behavioral realism distinguishes GBWM from conventional frameworks and makes it particularly suitable for the personalized wealth management context that Bank of America and Merrill Lynch have adopted in practice.

The integration of LLMs into this framework operates at three distinct levels. First, at the goal elicitation level, LLMs serve as a natural language interface that translates investors' informal, often vague descriptions of their financial objectives into structured parameters required by the GBWM model. For instance, when an investor says "I want to retire at 60 with enough passive income to cover my living expenses, and my child will need about 150,000 dollars for graduate school in five years," the LLM extracts and structures this into quantifiable goal specifications including time horizons, target amounts, priority weights, and minimum achievement probability thresholds. This is achieved through carefully designed prompts combined with JSON Schema constraints enforced via Pydantic validation, ensuring that the LLM output is both reliable and directly consumable by the downstream optimization engine. Second, at the market signal extraction level, the LLM processes unstructured text data such as FOMC statements, CPI reports, and analyst research notes to extract directional views on asset classes, which serve as auxiliary inputs for dynamically updating the market parameter estimates (expected returns and volatilities) within each goal-specific sub-portfolio. Third, at the result explanation level, the LLM generates natural language narratives that translate the quantitative model outputs, including portfolio weights, achievement probabilities, and rebalancing recommendations, into investor-friendly explanations. This addresses a critical gap in traditional quantitative models, where the outputs are often incomprehensible to non-specialist investors, thereby limiting the practical deployability of robo-advisory systems.

From an implementation perspective, I am designing a three-layer architecture. The interaction layer handles all investor-facing communication through the Shiny Dashboard, capturing natural language inputs and presenting structured goal confirmations. The modeling layer implements the GBWM optimization engine, solving each goal-specific sub-problem using Monte Carlo simulation combined with grid search for approximate solutions, and for simpler cases, the analytical approximations provided by Das et al. The asset universe will consist of approximately 10 to 15 representative ETFs spanning equities, fixed income, commodities, and real estate, using daily or weekly historical data obtained through yfinance and FRED for backtesting. The signal layer introduces lightweight machine learning models, specifically gradient-boosted trees, to provide short-term forecasts of asset-class returns and volatilities, which serve as dynamic updates to the GBWM market parameters rather than as standalone decision signals. This auxiliary role preserves the theoretical integrity of the GBWM framework while allowing data-driven parameter refinement. Benchmark comparisons will include the classic 60/40 portfolio, equal-weight allocation, and target-date fund glide paths, with performance evaluated across multiple market regimes including bull, bear, and high-volatility periods. The primary evaluation metric will be goal achievement probability, which is unique to the GBWM framework, supplemented by standard risk-return metrics such as Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, and Sortino ratio for cross-model comparability.

I believe this approach offers a compelling combination of theoretical rigor, practical relevance, and innovation. The GBWM framework is well-established in the academic literature and directly mirrors the goals-based advisory approach used at major wealth management firms. The LLM integration adds a layer of intelligent personalization that goes beyond traditional questionnaire-based profiling, and the multi-layer architecture ensures modularity and testability. ## Anticipated Discussion Points and Prepared Responses


**On the computational feasibility of GBWM with multiple assets and goals:** The key insight from Das et al. (2020) is that the multi-goal optimization problem decomposes into independent single-goal sub-problems under certain conditions, which dramatically reduces computational complexity. Each sub-problem involves optimizing over 10 to 15 assets for a single goal, which is well within the capability of Monte Carlo simulation with a reasonable number of scenarios (e.g., 10,000 paths). I plan to implement this using numpy's random Generator for reproducible simulation and Polars for efficient data processing, following the project's coding standards.

**On how LLM reliability is ensured for financial applications:** The LLM output is constrained by JSON Schema to ensure structural correctness, then validated by Pydantic models with business-rule checks (e.g., time horizons must be positive, achievement probabilities must fall between 50 and 99 percent, investment amounts must be positive). If validation fails, the system triggers a retry with refined prompts or returns an error to the Dashboard requesting the investor to clarify their input. This design ensures that no unvalidated LLM output ever reaches the quantitative optimization layer.

**On the role of machine learning in the framework:** Machine learning plays a deliberately auxiliary role in my design. I use gradient-boosted trees for short-term return and volatility forecasting at the asset-class level, and these forecasts serve as dynamic parameter updates for the GBWM model rather than as direct portfolio weight determinants. This design choice preserves the theoretical integrity of the GBWM framework while allowing data-driven refinement. I consciously avoided positioning ML as the core decision engine because the project's emphasis is on personalized goal-based investing, and GBWM provides a more interpretable and theoretically grounded foundation for that purpose.

**On benchmark selection and evaluation methodology:** Beyond the standard 60/40 and equal-weight benchmarks, I plan to include target-date fund glide paths as a benchmark because they represent the most widely adopted goals-based investment product in practice, providing a natural comparison point for GBWM. The evaluation will cover multiple market regimes to test robustness, and I will conduct ablation studies comparing the full framework against a version without LLM signal integration to quantify the marginal contribution of the LLM components.

**On data availability and potential obstacles:** The primary data sources, yfinance for ETF prices and FRED for macroeconomic indicators, are freely accessible and well-documented. The main risk is that some alternative ETFs may have shorter histories than desired. My mitigation strategy is to use corresponding index data as a proxy for ETFs with limited histories, and to conduct sensitivity analyses on the impact of sample period selection. For the LLM component, I plan to start with FOMC statements as the primary text data source, which are publicly available and cover a sufficiently long time period.
