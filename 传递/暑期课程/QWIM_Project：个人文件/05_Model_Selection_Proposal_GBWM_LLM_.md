# Model Selection Proposal: An Integrated Framework for Goals-Based Wealth Management and Large Language Models

After systematically reviewing the project's reference literature — with particular attention to Section 1.3.2 (Goal-based Investing) and Section 1.3.3 (Personalized Investing) — and aligning with our team's overall research plan, I have chosen Goals-Based Wealth Management (GBWM) as the core quantitative framework and propose to explore its deep integration with Large Language Models (LLMs). ## Project Understanding and Research Positioning

The central thesis of this project is to investigate personalized portfolio construction for individual investors within a quantitative wealth management framework. The project requires us to focus on mid-term (3-18 months) and long-term (18+ months) multi-asset portfolio optimization scenarios, using daily or lower-frequency financial time series data, and to benchmark our approaches against carefully selected reference strategies. What distinguishes this project is the dual requirement that each team member independently implements a quantitative model while collectively exploring the application potential of Large Language Models in personalized investing.

At its core, the research question we must address is: how can quantitative portfolio models be made not only mathematically optimal but genuinely aligned with each investor's individualized needs? Traditional portfolio optimization frameworks, such as the Markowitz mean-variance paradigm, pursue a single "optimal" solution. In practice, however, investors face far more complex decision environments — they simultaneously hold multiple investment goals (retirement planning, children's education, home purchase, etc.), each with its own time horizon, priority, and acceptable risk level. This is precisely the problem GBWM was designed to address, and it represents the area where integration with LLMs can deliver the greatest value.

My research positioning is as follows: to construct a complete closed-loop system comprising "LLM-driven needs interpretation → parametric modeling → GBWM dynamic optimization → explainable result presentation," and to validate its effectiveness in mid-to-long-term multi-asset portfolio scenarios.

## Theoretical Advantages of GBWM and Selection Rationale

The selection of GBWM as the core model is grounded in several considerations. First, there is a high degree of thematic alignment with the project. The project is titled "Personalized Wealth Portfolios," and GBWM's foundational design philosophy is investor-goal-centricity. Unlike traditional portfolio optimization, which derives optimal weights from aggregate market behavior, GBWM treats each of an investor's specific goals as an independent "mental account," each with its own investment horizon, target amount, and achievement probability requirement. Personalization is thus not an external constraint imposed upon the model, but rather an intrinsic driving force of the model architecture.

Second, the theoretical foundation is robust. The works of Das, Evans, and Ashvin (2020, 2022) provide a rigorous stochastic optimal control framework for GBWM, proving that under certain conditions, multi-objective optimization problems can be decomposed into independent single-objective sub-problems, each associated with an S-shaped Prospect (SP) utility function. This decomposition property is not only theoretically elegant but also substantially reduces computational complexity in practice.

Third, there is direct practical relevance. Goals-Based Investing has become the core client-service framework at institutions including Bank of America and Merrill Lynch. Selecting GBWM ensures that our research outputs carry immediate industry applicability — a consideration of particular significance for a project mentored by a Bank of America quantitative wealth management specialist.

Fourth, GBWM inherently supports dynamic rebalancing. Traditional MVO is fundamentally a single-period static model, whereas GBWM natively accommodates multi-period dynamic asset allocation — as time progresses and market conditions evolve, the model dynamically adjusts allocation weights across goal-specific accounts. This dynamic capability aligns closely with the project's emphasis on mid-to-long-term investment horizons spanning three months to several years.

## The Role and Advantages of LLMs in This Framework

Large Language Models in this framework do not serve as standalone prediction tools but rather as a bridging layer connecting investors' unstructured requirements to the structured parameters required by quantitative models. Their advantages manifest across several dimensions.

At the natural language understanding level, investors typically express their investment goals in vague, unstructured language — for example, "I'd like to have sufficient passive income each month after retirement" or "My child will need graduate school tuition in a few years, and that money can't be at risk." LLMs can accurately extract structured parameters such as time horizon, target amount, risk tolerance, and priority from such natural language expressions. These parameters are precisely the inputs required by the GBWM model. Compared to traditional questionnaire-based risk assessment (which is inherently limited by pre-designed questions and fixed response options), an LLM-driven multi-turn dialogue can more naturally and deeply uncover an investor's true needs.

At the information augmentation level, LLMs can process unstructured text data — macroeconomic reports, central bank statements, analyst research notes — and extract signals relevant to asset allocation decisions (inflation expectations, interest rate outlook, sector sentiment, etc.). These signals provide supplementary inputs for the market parameter estimation within the GBWM model.

At the result explanation level, the outputs of quantitative models (portfolio weights, achievement probabilities, risk metrics) are often difficult for non-specialist investors to interpret. LLMs can translate these numerical results into natural language narratives that investors can understand — explaining why the current allocation is structured a certain way, how the strategy adjusts when market conditions shift, and what a declining achievement probability for a particular goal implies. This explainability is critical for the practical deployment of any robo-advisory system.

## Integrated Framework Design and Implementation Approach

The overall framework is designed as a three-layer architecture: an Interaction Layer (LLM-driven), a Modeling Layer (GBWM core), and a Signal Layer (machine learning enhanced).

The Interaction Layer manages all communication with the investor. Investors describe their goals and preferences in natural language, and the LLM progressively clarifies and refines these requirements through multi-turn dialogue. The LLM's output is a structured "Goals Manifest," where each goal contains the following fields: investment horizon T (years), target amount W_target, minimum achievement probability threshold p_min, priority weight λ, and goal type (lump-sum expenditure or sustained cash flow). To implement this layer, I plan to employ structured prompt engineering combined with JSON schema constraints on LLM output format, ensuring the reliability and consistency of extracted parameters. A confirmation mechanism will also be designed — the LLM presents its parsed results back to the investor in natural language for verification and correction, forming a closed loop.

The Modeling Layer constitutes the core computational engine of GBWM. Upon receiving the Goals Manifest, the system allocates the investor's total wealth across goal-specific accounts. Each sub-account independently undergoes dynamic portfolio optimization, with the objective of maximizing the goal achievement probability P(W_T ≥ W_target) within that account's investment horizon. Specifically, the utility function for each sub-problem adopts the S-shaped form proposed by Das et al.: investors exhibit risk-seeking behavior below the target amount (willing to take on more risk to achieve the goal) and risk-averse behavior above the target amount (protecting accumulated gains). For solution methodology, I plan to employ Monte Carlo simulation combined with grid search for approximate solutions; for simpler sub-problems, the analytical approximations provided by Das et al. may also be utilized. The asset universe will consist of 5-15 representative ETFs spanning equities, fixed income, commodities, and REITs, using daily or weekly historical data for backtesting. Benchmark comparisons will include the classic 60/40 portfolio, equal-weight portfolio, and target-date fund glide paths.

The Signal Layer introduces moderately lightweight machine learning methods to enhance the model's market parameter estimation capability. Specifically, I plan to use relatively parsimonious ML models (such as gradient-boosted trees or a simple LSTM network) for short-term return and volatility forecasting at the asset-class level. These forecasts serve as dynamic update sources for market parameters within the GBWM model. The design principle for this layer is "auxiliary, not dominant" — ML predictions are used to fine-tune model parameters rather than directly determine portfolio weights, thereby preserving the theoretical integrity of the GBWM framework. This layer also provides a quantitative conversion interface for text-based signals extracted by the LLM: qualitative judgments such as "rising inflation expectations" extracted from macroeconomic texts can be translated, via the Signal Layer, into adjustments to bond yield parameters.

For Dashboard integration, each model will be presented as an independent Tab within the Shiny Dashboard. My Tab will include the following Sub-tabs: (1) Goal Setting Interface — investors input goals via dialogue or forms, with the LLM parsing and displaying structured results in real time; (2) Portfolio Allocation Display — current allocation weights and dynamic adjustment history for each goal-specific account; (3) Achievement Probability Tracking — current achievement probabilities for each goal with time-series trajectories; (4) Performance Comparison — comparative analysis against benchmark portfolios across metrics including return, volatility, Sharpe ratio, and maximum drawdown.

## Anticipated Challenges and Mitigation Strategies

The first challenge concerns the computational complexity of GBWM. Multi-objective dynamic optimization problems theoretically face the "curse of dimensionality," particularly when the number of assets is large, causing dynamic programming solution costs to escalate rapidly. The mitigation strategy leverages the decomposability property proven by Das et al., breaking multi-objective problems into multiple independent single-objective sub-problems, each with substantially reduced dimensionality. Additionally, Monte Carlo simulation will be used as an alternative to rigorous dynamic programming, striking a balance between precision and computational efficiency.

The second challenge involves the consistency and reliability of LLM outputs. LLMs may produce varying outputs for identical inputs, which is unacceptable in financial applications. Mitigation strategies include enforcing JSON schema output constraints, implementing multi-round confirmation mechanisms, and applying reasonable range validation on critical parameters (e.g., investment horizons capped at 40 years, achievement probabilities bounded between 50%-99%). During the model evaluation phase, I will systematically test the consistency of the LLM's parsing of identical goals expressed in different linguistic formulations.

The third challenge pertains to backtesting data acquisition and quality. Multi-asset long-horizon backtesting requires high-quality historical data, and certain asset classes (such as some alternative investment ETFs) have limited histories. The mitigation strategy involves using data sources such as yfinance to obtain long-run daily data for mainstream ETFs, substituting index-level historical data for asset classes with shorter ETF histories. Sensitivity analyses will evaluate the impact of data length and sample period selection on conclusions.

The fourth challenge involves the engineering complexity of integrating LLMs with traditional models. Seamlessly connecting the asynchronous API calls of the LLM interaction layer with the GBWM computational engine and Dashboard framework may present technical challenges. The mitigation strategy adopts a layered decoupling architecture — the LLM interaction layer's output is standardized in JSON format and communicates with the GBWM computational layer through explicitly defined interfaces, reducing coupling. Integration testing will begin early rather than being deferred to the final stages.

The fifth challenge relates to demonstrating model persuasiveness. The project requires not only model implementation but also convincing the audience of the selected model's advantages and practical utility. This demands well-designed numerical experiments. I plan to demonstrate persuasiveness along the following dimensions: robustness analysis across different market regimes (bull, bear, and high-volatility periods); personalization effectiveness across different investor profiles (young aggressive, mid-career balanced, near-retirement conservative); and ablation studies comparing the full framework against purely traditional methods (without LLM augmentation), quantifying the marginal contribution of LLM-derived signals.
